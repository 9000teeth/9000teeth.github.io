I showed him the video of Lang Lang, who he finds tedious, performing with his father, and he told me that his own brother had owned an erhu once. They cost two yuan, a thirtieth of his family’s monthly income. They were easy to produce (one side covered with snakeskin / one side open) and sounded, most importantly, sad, and so were a popular instrument with beggars and buskers. Like Irish pennywhistles.

We share a conversation every time we’re in the car, but it is always what he tells me about the country only a decade and a half old when he knew it that I enjoy most. A smooth, journalistic feeling to the way he speaks. When we went to the park on Friday, he talked about dragonflies / powderflies / how kids would eat milkweed, proclaiming it nourishing. I replied with how the kids I grew up with split dandelion stems, drank the milk for bravery.

I do not want to tell him this: I feel that he’ll die before I graduate college. Suddenly. Not an accident. If he falls terminally ill, I’ll buy a notebook with a cover design he’ll like (cats / sunflowers / powderflies) from the Japanese stationary store we visit on trips south. I’ll ask him to recount as much of his life as he can tolerate. The childhoods especially, springtime portended by red dragonflies;

then he’ll keep talking, but not about his childhood at all. About, maybe, why Tianjin is the worst-designed city in the world, but how yuxiang meat is as popular there as burgers in America; how unclean the streets are, but how commuters ate lamian served from an enormous pot into bowls that they held squatting in the grass. Or about war: Foreign soldiers made corpse-piles in times of siege, in every public grave a corpse-pile,

starvation so rampant that for every five men withered to death there was one who gorged himself on weeds until his stomach ruptured; how after months of starvation, somebody opened a bao shop, and the question nobody asked was, Where is the meat coming from?, but...

I don’t know if there’s a second half after the “but”. What I mean is that he’ll talk about this and that and I’ll be listening so intently that I’ll close the notebook, promise to fill in the rest from memory later. Then I’ll get too busy to visit his hospital room.

Is it appropriate to say I don’t remember how this was supposed to end if it won’t even happen?

1 jan 2022

Back home for a day and a night and part of a day. Not moving, just passing through. Most of my belongings are still in the car. Chicago is snowless, and here the blizzard has so thickened that you can really only feel either comforted or hopeless.

I began thinking up obituaries for myself:

(Claire/my childhood name) (x), aged (x), was found dead this morning of a self-inflicted gunshot wound. Traces of the prescription sedative Xanax were found in (his/her) bloodstream. Absent contravening evidence... well, no, I can’t say that. This isn’t a crime scene.

Mom was happy to see me. I was sleeping in my childhood bed in my childhood bedroom, which seemed smaller than ever. I thought it was my new heeled boots, but even after taking them off, I couldn’t fathom having spent every day of my life in that room.

I woke up but I didn’t move. Someone stopped in the doorway—I thought it was my mom. It was my dad, and he sighed very quietly and left. I can only tell you this with any certainty because he used to do this all the time, if you could believe me.

When I finally got out of bed, I thought it was morning, but I went to the kitchen and saw that it was ten past one in the afternoon.

31 dec 2021

Today, YouTube’s algorithm favors long videos that flower into long retention times, such that video essayists—running the gamut from ostensibly political to outwardly so—have gained favor among a generation which grew up with easy access to multimedia forms of expression and, now in their late teens and early twenties, takes a certain interest in these forms as education.

The lifestyle vloggers must be on Instagram now, or else they are dead. In southern California waving, smiling, shutting off their cameras. What they never show are the long hours afterward of sitting alone to rewatch and edit and critique, silently, these scenes, which are morbid in a way we all recognize too intimately to call morbid.

I once saw a video diary in which the editor made a mistake: They left in footage of the vlogger boring holes through their viewfinder, sighing at times, eager (if I could call it eager) to get on to the next exciting scene. The light from the computer monitor was harsh on their face, and in that grainy half-moment I did not recall seeing the lens reflected back in their eyes.
